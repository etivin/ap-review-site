Here is a full AP World History narrative with each unit as its own paragraph and key terms bolded throughout:

Unit 1: The Global Tapestry (1200–1450)
From 1200 to 1450, major societies across the world developed distinctive political systems while still sharing common patterns of state-building. In **Song China**, rulers relied on **Confucianism**, the **imperial bureaucracy**, and the **scholar-gentry** to justify order, while innovations like **champa rice**, expansion of the **Grand Canal**, and growing commercialization strengthened the economy. In **Dar al-Islam**, the decline of the **Abbasid Caliphate** led to new states like the **Mamluk Sultanate** and **Delhi Sultanates**, while **Islam** spread through conquest, trade, and the work of **Sufis**. In South and Southeast Asia, **Hinduism**, **Buddhism**, and **Islam** shaped states such as the **Khmer Empire**, **Majapahit**, and **Srivijaya**, while religious movements like **Bhakti** influenced daily life and monumental architecture such as **Angkor Wat**. In the Americas, the **Mexica** and **Inca** built powerful empires using systems like **tribute** and the **mit’a**. In Africa, states such as **Mali**, **Ethiopia**, and **Great Zimbabwe** expanded through trade and regional power. Meanwhile, Europe remained politically fragmented under **feudalism**, the **manorial system**, and decentralized monarchies, while **Christianity**, along with **Judaism** and **Islam**, continued to shape European society. Across all these regions, states showed both **continuity and innovation**, blending old traditions with new forms of power.

Unit 2: Networks of Exchange (1200–1450)
Between 1200 and 1450, expanding trade networks connected Afro-Eurasia more deeply than ever before. The **Silk Roads**, **Indian Ocean trade network**, and **trans-Saharan trade routes** all grew because of improved transportation, commercial practices, and rising demand for luxury goods. Along the Silk Roads, technologies such as **caravanserai**, **paper money**, and systems of **credit** supported exchange, while goods like **textiles**, **porcelain**, and **steel** moved across continents. The rise of the **Mongol Empire** intensified these connections by making travel and trade safer and encouraging cultural and technological diffusion, including the transfer of **Greco-Islamic knowledge**. In the Indian Ocean, sailors used **monsoon winds**, the **compass**, the **astrolabe**, and improved ships to connect cities from East Africa to Southeast Asia, supporting states like **Malacca** and the **Swahili Coast** city-states. Across the Sahara, merchants used **camels** and caravans to link West Africa to the broader Afro-Eurasian economy, helping states like **Mali** prosper. These networks spread religion, technology, crops, and disease. Travelers such as **Ibn Battuta** and **Marco Polo** recorded these interconnections, while the spread of **bananas**, new rice strains, and the **bubonic plague** showed that exchange had environmental as well as cultural effects. Overall, trade networks transformed cities, societies, and ecosystems across the Eastern Hemisphere.

Unit 3: Land-Based Empires (1450–1750)
From 1450 to 1750, large **land-based empires** expanded through military power, especially the use of **gunpowder**, **cannons**, and professional armies. Major empires included the **Ottoman Empire**, the **Safavid Empire**, the **Mughal Empire**, and the **Manchu Qing Dynasty**. These states built powerful centralized governments by recruiting military elites, bureaucrats, and loyal administrators, such as the Ottoman **devshirme** system and peasant warriors like the **Cossacks**. Rulers legitimized their authority through religion, art, and monumental architecture, from **Versailles** in France to **Mughal mausolea** and **Qing imperial portraits**. Systems such as **tax farming** and **zamindar** tax collection helped these empires raise revenue and sustain expansion. At the same time, belief systems changed and adapted. The **Protestant Reformation** challenged the Catholic Church and transformed Christianity, while rivalry between the Sunni **Ottomans** and Shi’a **Safavids** deepened divisions within Islam. In South Asia, **Sikhism** emerged from interactions between **Hinduism** and **Islam**. These empires were both shaped by and influential over the diverse peoples they ruled, demonstrating that empire-building involved military conquest, political administration, and religious negotiation.

Unit 4: Transoceanic Interconnections (1450–1750)
Between 1450 and 1750, oceanic travel connected the Eastern and Western Hemispheres in new ways and created a truly global system. European exploration was made possible by technologies borrowed and improved from earlier societies, including the **lateen sail**, **compass**, **astrolabe**, **caravel**, and **fluyt**, along with better knowledge of wind and current patterns. **Portugal** built a trading-post empire in Africa and Asia, **Spain** sponsored **Columbus** and conquered large American empires, and **England**, **France**, and the **Dutch Republic** joined the race for overseas influence. These voyages sparked the **Columbian Exchange**, which transferred crops, animals, people, and diseases between hemispheres. American crops like **maize** and **potatoes** boosted populations in Afro-Eurasia, while Europeans brought **horses**, **cattle**, **sugar**, and devastating diseases such as **smallpox**, **measles**, and **malaria** to the Americas. European maritime empires established colonies and plantation economies that relied on systems like **encomienda**, **hacienda**, **indentured servitude**, and **chattel slavery**, while older systems like the **mit’a** were adapted for colonial labor. **Mercantilism** and **joint-stock companies** helped states maintain overseas empires, while the global flow of **silver**, especially from Spanish America to China, tied world markets together. This era transformed labor systems, intensified the **Atlantic slave trade**, created new **syncretic** cultures, and reshaped global social hierarchies.

Unit 5: Revolutions (1750–1900)
From 1750 to 1900, revolutions in politics, thought, and industry transformed societies around the world. The **Enlightenment** promoted **reason**, **natural rights**, and the **social contract**, encouraging people to challenge monarchy, tradition, and inherited privilege. Thinkers inspired revolutionary movements such as the **American Revolution**, **French Revolution**, **Haitian Revolution**, and **Latin American independence movements**, all of which drew on ideas expressed in documents like the **Declaration of Independence**, the **Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen**, and **Bolívar’s Letter from Jamaica**. At the same time, **nationalism** united people around shared language, culture, and territory, contributing to new nation-states and unifications such as **Germany** and **Italy**. Meanwhile, the **Industrial Revolution** began in **England**, fueled by coal, iron, waterways, urbanization, capital, and agricultural improvements. The **factory system** concentrated labor and increased productivity, while machines like the **steam engine** and later the **internal combustion engine** transformed manufacturing and transportation. Industrialization spread to Europe, the United States, Russia, and Japan, helping some regions dominate global production. New economic ideas emerged, including **laissez-faire capitalism**, **free trade**, **socialism**, and **communism**, while industrial society gave rise to **labor unions**, urban reform, and critiques by thinkers like **Karl Marx**. Industrialization increased wealth and consumer goods for some, but also caused harsh working conditions, class conflict, and dramatic social change.

Unit 6: Consequences of Industrialization (1750–1900)
As industrialization accelerated, powerful states sought raw materials, labor, and markets through a new wave of **imperialism**. European empires, along with the **United States** and **Japan**, justified expansion with ideologies such as **Social Darwinism**, **nationalism**, the **civilizing mission**, and missionary Christianity. Imperial states expanded through warfare, diplomacy, and settlement, seizing territories across Africa, Asia, and the Pacific. In some cases, colonial control shifted from private hands to governments, as seen in the **Congo Free State** under **King Leopold II** and later Belgium. Imperial economies pushed colonized regions into **export economies**, producing raw materials like **cotton**, **rubber**, and **diamonds** for industrial powers. **Economic imperialism** also gave industrialized nations major influence without full political control, as seen in the **Opium Wars** in China. These pressures sparked resistance, including the **Indian Rebellion of 1857**, the rise of the **Zulu Kingdom**, and the **Xhosa Cattle-Killing Movement**. Industrialization also drove global migration, both voluntary and coerced. Millions moved in search of work, including the **Irish** to the United States, while systems of **indentured servitude**, contract labor, and other coercive migration moved **Chinese** and **Indian** laborers worldwide. Migrants formed ethnic enclaves but also faced racism and exclusion, such as the **Chinese Exclusion Act** and the **White Australia policy**. By 1900, industrial capitalism and imperial expansion had deeply reorganized global labor, migration, and political power.

Unit 7: Global Conflict (1900–present)
The 20th century was defined by dramatic shifts in power, revolution, and catastrophic warfare. At the century’s start, Western empires dominated much of the world, but old empires such as the **Ottoman**, **Russian**, and **Qing** collapsed under internal weaknesses and external pressures. The **Mexican Revolution** and the **Russian Revolution** reflected wider global unrest. **World War I** erupted because of **militarism**, **alliances**, **imperialism**, and **nationalism**, becoming the first major **total war**, in which governments used **propaganda**, mass mobilization, and industrial weaponry to fight on an unprecedented scale. After the war, the **Great Depression** led governments to intervene more actively in economic life through programs like the **New Deal**, **fascist corporatism**, and **Five-Year Plans** in the Soviet Union. Unresolved tensions, punitive treaties, economic collapse, and aggressive **fascist** regimes led to **World War II**, another total war marked by **fire-bombing**, the **atomic bomb**, genocide, and even greater destruction. The **Holocaust**, in which Nazi Germany attempted the extermination of Europe’s Jews, stands as the most infamous mass atrocity of the era, alongside the **Armenian Genocide** and other campaigns of ethnic violence. These global conflicts showed how modern technology, extremist ideology, and state power could combine to produce devastation on an unprecedented scale.

Unit 8: Cold War and Decolonization (1900–present)
After World War II, the world entered a new era shaped by **decolonization** and the **Cold War**. European empires weakened, and anti-imperialist movements grew stronger, leading colonies in Asia, Africa, and the Middle East to seek independence. Some gained freedom through negotiation, as in **India** and **Ghana**, while others fought wars of liberation, as in **Algeria** and **Vietnam**. New nations emerged, including **Pakistan** and **Israel**, though the redrawing of borders often caused violence and displacement, especially during the **Partition of India** and conflicts in the Middle East. At the same time, the global balance of power shifted to the **United States** and the **Soviet Union**, whose ideological rivalry between **capitalism** and **communism** defined the Cold War. This struggle produced alliances such as **NATO** and the **Warsaw Pact**, as well as **proxy wars** in places like **Angola**, **Nicaragua**, and Vietnam. In China, Communist victory led to major social and economic transformation under policies such as the **Great Leap Forward**. Some nations tried to avoid choosing sides through the **Non-Aligned Movement**, led by figures like **Sukarno** and **Kwame Nkrumah**. Individuals such as **Mohandas Gandhi**, **Martin Luther King Jr.**, and **Nelson Mandela** challenged power through nonviolence, while others turned to revolutionary violence or authoritarian rule. The Cold War finally ended because of economic weakness in the Soviet bloc, public dissatisfaction, the Soviet failure in **Afghanistan**, and U.S. technological and military pressure, leading to the collapse of the Soviet Union.

Unit 9: Globalization (1900–present)
Since 1900, and especially since the late 20th century, the world has become increasingly interconnected through **globalization**. New technologies such as **radio**, **cellular communication**, the **internet**, **air travel**, and **shipping containers** reduced geographic distance and accelerated the movement of ideas, goods, and people. Advances in **petroleum**, **nuclear power**, **vaccines**, **antibiotics**, and **birth control** transformed standards of living, health, and family life, while the **Green Revolution** increased food production and helped sustain a growing global population. At the same time, globalization intensified environmental challenges, including **deforestation**, **desertification**, air pollution, water scarcity, and debates over **climate change** caused by **greenhouse gases**. Economically, many governments embraced **free-market reforms**, from **Margaret Thatcher** in Britain to **Deng Xiaoping** in China, while manufacturing shifted increasingly to Asia and Latin America and **knowledge economies** grew in places like the United States, Japan, and Finland. **Multinational corporations**, trade agreements like **NAFTA**, and institutions such as the **United Nations** reflected this new global order. Socially and culturally, globalization spread global brands, entertainment, and popular culture, from **Bollywood** and **Reggae** to **Coca-Cola**, **McDonald’s**, and social media. Yet globalization also sparked resistance, including **anti-IMF** and **anti-World Bank** activism, and encouraged reform movements for human rights, environmental justice, and equality, including the **Universal Declaration of Human Rights**, global feminism, and the **Green Belt Movement**. In the modern era, globalization has brought both unprecedented opportunity and deep inequality, linking the world more closely while also creating new tensions.
